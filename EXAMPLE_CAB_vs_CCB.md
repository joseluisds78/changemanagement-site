# CAB vs CCB: Understanding the Difference

**Published:** January 15, 2024 | **Read time:** 8 minutes

---

## Introduction

One of the most common sources of confusion in change management is the difference between CAB and CCB. Are they the same thing? Do you need both? Should you use one instead of the other?

The answer depends on your industry and the type of changes you're managing.

---

## Quick Answer

**CAB = Change Advisory Board** (used in IT Service Management, ITIL)
**CCB = Change Control Board** (used in Engineering, Project Management, and Aerospace)

They serve similar purposes but have different contexts, terminology, and sometimes different scope.

---

## What is a CAB?

A **Change Advisory Board (CAB)** is an ITIL concept used primarily in IT Service Management.

### CAB Characteristics:
- **Context:** IT Service Management (ITSM)
- **Focus:** Managing changes to IT services
- **Scope:** Software, infrastructure, systems, applications
- **Participants:** IT managers, service owners, business representatives, stakeholders
- **Decision:** Approve/reject changes based on risk and business impact
- **Frequency:** Varies (weekly, bi-weekly, or as-needed)

### What a CAB Assesses:
- Impact on users and services
- Risk to system stability
- Resource requirements
- Timeline feasibility
- Rollback plans
- Stakeholder alignment

### CAB Governance (ITIL):
- Part of the Change Management process
- Mandatory for standard and emergency changes
- Documented decisions
- Integration with incident management
- Metrics tracking (success rate, approval time, delays)

---

## What is a CCB?

A **Change Control Board (CCB)** is commonly used in Engineering, Aerospace, Defense, and large-scale Project Management.

### CCB Characteristics:
- **Context:** Engineering, Aerospace, Defense, Project Management
- **Focus:** Controlling technical changes and baselines
- **Scope:** Hardware, designs, specifications, configurations, documentation
- **Participants:** Engineers, project managers, technical leads, quality assurance
- **Decision:** Approve/reject changes based on technical and contractual impact
- **Frequency:** Typically scheduled or as changes are submitted

### What a CCB Assesses:
- Impact on design specifications
- Risk to system performance and safety
- Configuration baseline impact
- Documentation consistency
- Regulatory compliance
- Cost implications
- Technical feasibility

### CCB Governance (Engineering):
- Part of Configuration Management
- Baseline protection (design, technical, product)
- Configuration item tracking
- Traceability documentation
- Change request forms and processes
- Impact analysis required

---

## Key Differences

| Aspect | CAB | CCB |
|--------|-----|-----|
| **Industry** | IT Service Management | Engineering, Aerospace, Defense |
| **Focus** | Service continuity | Technical baseline control |
| **Scope** | IT services, applications | Hardware, designs, specifications |
| **Framework** | ITIL | Configuration Management, CMMI |
| **Primary Concern** | Operational risk | Technical integrity |
| **Documentation** | Change records, metrics | Change requests, baselines |
| **Participants** | IT ops, service owners, business | Engineers, tech leads, QA |
| **Decision Criteria** | Business impact, risk | Technical impact, specifications |

---

## The "Same Board" Scenario

In many **enterprise organizations**, especially those dealing with both IT and engineering, a single board might handle both IT changes (CAB concept) and engineering changes (CCB concept).

### You might hear:
- "Our CCB meets weekly to approve all changes"
- "We call it CAB but manage it like a CCB"
- "We use CAB for IT changes and CCB for infrastructure projects"

This is normal. The terminology reflects the industry and organizational structure.

---

## Real Examples

### Example 1: Financial Institution

**Using CAB (ITIL):**
- Approves deployment of new banking applications
- Reviews impact on trading systems
- Assesses customer-facing changes
- Tracks change success rates (99.9% target)
- Manages emergency changes during market hours

**Why:** IT-focused, service-oriented, ITIL framework is standard in banking.

---

### Example 2: Semiconductor Company (like NXP)

**Using CCB (Engineering):**
- Controls changes to chip design specifications
- Manages hardware configuration baselines
- Reviews impact on production processes
- Ensures regulatory compliance (aerospace, automotive specs)
- Tracks change impact on cost and timeline

**Why:** Engineering-heavy, specification-driven, critical hardware/design baseline protection.

---

### Example 3: Large Enterprise (like NATO or Amadeus)

**Using Both:**
- **CAB:** Approves IT system changes, network updates, software deployments
- **CCB:** Controls configuration baselines, infrastructure specifications, technical documentation

**Why:** Multi-disciplinary organization with both IT service management and engineering concerns.

---

## How to Know Which You Need

### Use CAB if:
✓ You're primarily managing IT services and applications  
✓ You follow ITIL framework  
✓ Your main concern is service continuity and user impact  
✓ You have rapid change cycles (multiple changes per week)  
✓ You track metrics like success rate, approval time, and incident correlation  

### Use CCB if:
✓ You're managing engineering/hardware changes  
✓ You need strict baseline control  
✓ Your changes have long approval cycles  
✓ Regulatory/certification requirements exist  
✓ Configuration traceability is critical  

### Use Both if:
✓ You have complex IT infrastructure with engineering considerations  
✓ You manage both applications AND infrastructure  
✓ You operate in highly regulated industries  
✓ You have distributed teams with different disciplines

## Conclusion

CAB and CCB are not competing concepts—they're domain-specific approaches to change governance:

- **CAB** = IT Service Management focus (ITIL)
- **CCB** = Engineering/Technical focus (Configuration Management)

Your organization might call it one or the other, but what matters is that you have a **structured governance process** that:
- Assesses impact and risk
- Gets buy-in from stakeholders
- Documents decisions
- Tracks metrics
- Continuously improves

Whether you call it CAB, CCB, or "Change Review Board," the principle is the same: **controlled change delivery reduces risk and improves outcomes.**
